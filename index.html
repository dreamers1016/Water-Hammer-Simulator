What Is Water Hammer?

Sudden momentum change in confined fluids

When a valve closes rapidly, the flowing fluid decelerates abruptly. Kinetic energy converts to a pressure surge travelling upstream as an acoustic wave — the audible "hammer" in plumbing systems. The wave reflects back and forth between the valve and the supply reservoir, oscillating until friction dissipates the energy.

ΔP = ρ · a · Δv
(Joukowski equation, 1898)
Wave Speed Formula (Korteweg, 1878)

Acoustic velocity — fluid compressibility + pipe elasticity

Wave speed is set by how compressible the fluid is and how much the pipe wall can flex. Rigid steel barely deforms, so the wave travels near the acoustic speed of water (~1484 m/s). Flexible PVC absorbs deformation, slowing the wave dramatically and reducing peak pressure — but at the cost of pipe flexibility fatigue.

a = √[ K/ρ / (1 + K·d / (E·t)) ]
K=bulk mod, E=Young's mod, d=diameter, t=wall thickness
Chemical Engineering Applications

Where pressure transients matter in process plants

Water hammer occurs in: cooling water networks (pump trip/ESD events), steam condensate lines (steam collapse + liquid slug), chemical dosing systems (check-valve slam), and crude oil pipelines (fast block-valve actuation). Uncontrolled surges cause pipe rupture, flange leaks, valve seat damage, and instrument impulse-line failures.

T_critical = 2L / a
Closure faster than T_critical → full Joukowski ΔP
